Switching to Tech in Your 30s (or 40s): What Actually Happens

The most common question on our advisor calls isn't about courses, curriculum or price. It's some version of "I'm 34/38/45 — is it too late?" We've now watched thousands of over-30 career-switchers go through this transition, so instead of a motivational poster, here's what actually happens.

What age actually costs you

Let's not pretend it's nothing. Compared to a 22-year-old, you likely have less free time, more financial obligations, and you'll occasionally be interviewed by someone a decade younger — which stings until it doesn't. Ageism exists in some corners of tech, particularly in trend-chasing startups. Junior salaries may also be a pay cut from a career you've built for fifteen years, and that math needs honest attention before you leap.

What age buys you (more than you think)

Here's what our older graduates consistently report as advantages: you know how to work. You've survived deadlines, difficult stakeholders and office politics — things that derail younger juniors constantly. Domain expertise compounds: a nurse who learns data analytics is instantly more valuable in health-tech than a generic analyst; an ex-teacher who learns UX understands users professionally. Hiring managers repeatedly tell us the same thing: they can teach syntax, they can't teach professionalism. And discipline — the thing that kills most self-taught journeys — is precisely what fifteen working years builds.

The salary math, honestly

A first tech role may match or dip below a senior non-tech salary. The difference is trajectory: tech compensation curves upward fast in the first five years, while most non-tech careers have plateaued by mid-30s. Most of our over-35 graduates who took a small initial cut passed their old salary within 18–30 months. Model it over five years, not one — and consider internal switches (new skills, same employer), which often skip the cut entirely.

The 12-month plan that survives real life

  1. Months 1–2: Test before you invest. Free resources, 5 hours a week. The goal isn't progress; it's discovering whether you can sustain interest when it gets boring. Some can't — better to learn that for free.
  2. Months 3–8: Structured learning, 8–10 hrs/week. Early mornings beat late nights for people with families — willpower is a morning resource. One track, one stack, no shopping around mid-way.
  3. Months 6–10 (overlapping): Build public proof. Portfolio projects, ideally aimed at your existing industry — that intersection is your unfair advantage.
  4. Months 9–12: Apply while "not ready." Target 20% internal opportunities, 80% external. Career-changer resumes need interviews to calibrate; the first five applications are practice, treat them that way.

The pattern in those who make it

After thousands of these journeys, the successful ones share three traits: they treated learning like a standing appointment (not a mood), they told people about the switch early (accountability beats secrecy), and they didn't go alone — a mentor, cohort or community carried them through month four, which is where everyone wants to quit. Age never appears on that list. The oldest graduate we've placed was 54, and her hiring manager's comment was: "finally, someone who runs a meeting properly."
