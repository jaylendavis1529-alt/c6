The Tech Resume That Gets Interviews (With Real Examples)

Our career services team has reviewed north of 10,000 resumes, and the difference between "auto-rejected" and "interview scheduled" is rarely the candidate — it's the document. Here's everything we teach graduates, condensed.

Survive the two gates

Your resume faces two filters: an ATS (applicant tracking system) that scans for keywords, then a human who gives it roughly seven seconds. The ATS wants standard section headers, real text (no tables or graphics), and the actual words from the job posting. The human wants to see, instantly: what you can do, proof you've done it, and zero friction finding either. Every rule below serves one of those two readers.

The structure for career-changers

  1. Header: name, city, email, phone, LinkedIn, GitHub/portfolio link. No photo, no full address, no "objective."
  2. Summary (2–3 lines, not a paragraph): "Customer-support professional turned front-end developer with three deployed React applications" beats any adjective soup. State the transition confidently — don't apologize for it.
  3. Skills: a clean scannable list of technologies, grouped (Languages / Frameworks / Tools). Only list what you can discuss in an interview — everything here is fair game for questions.
  4. Projects — your experience section: for career-changers, this goes ABOVE work history. Three strong projects with links, each described like a job.
  5. Work history: compressed, but mined for transferable gold (see below).
  6. Education & certifications: brief, at the bottom, unless it's your strongest card.

Write projects like achievements, not features

Weak: "Built a to-do app with React." Strong: "Built and deployed a task-management app (React, Node, PostgreSQL) with authentication and real-time sync; 40+ active users, Lighthouse performance 96." The formula is what you built + stack + evidence it's real (users, metrics, a live link). Numbers create credibility even at small scale — "40 users" reads as real; "passionate about clean code" reads as filler.

Mine your old career — don't bury it

Career-changers routinely hide their strongest asset. The retail manager "led a team of 12 and cut scheduling conflicts 60% by building a spreadsheet system" — that's leadership, analytics and process automation. Translate each old role into the language of your new field: numbers, systems, outcomes. Hiring managers hire adults who've succeeded somewhere; show them the pattern travels.

The auto-reject list

  • One resume for every job. Mirror each posting's key terms (honestly) — the ATS is literal.
  • Skill bars, star ratings, "80% JavaScript." Meaningless and mocked.
  • Two pages before you have five years' experience. One page. Yes, one.
  • Dead links. A broken portfolio link is worse than none.
  • Typos in a document claiming attention to detail. Read it aloud, then have someone else read it.

The final test

Hand your resume to someone for seven seconds, take it back, and ask what they remember. If the answer isn't "you build X and you've proven it," revise. The last 20% is having projects worth writing about, and that part you have to build.
